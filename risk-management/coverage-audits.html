Policies don't update themselves. Operations change. We audit every line of every policy at every renewal — most brokers don't.

What policy drift looks like

A client buys a $2M general liability program when they're a 12-employee operation doing $4M in revenue. Five years later, they're 45 employees, $18M in revenue, with three new locations and a new service line — and they're still on the same $2M policy with the same scheduled location list.

That's policy drift. It happens to almost every operation. And it's the work the broker is supposed to be doing — but most don't.

What we audit

Limits — are they sized to current revenue, asset value, contract requirements, and risk profile?

Named insured — is every entity properly named, including new subsidiaries, DBAs, and acquired operations?

Locations — every location, with current building values and contents values?

Class codes — for workers' comp and BOPs, are class codes accurate to current operations?

Endorsements — are all needed endorsements in place (additional insured forms, waivers of subrogation, primary/non-contributory)?

Exclusions — are there carrier-added exclusions that have crept in over time and shouldn't be there?

Sub-limits — particularly on cyber, equipment breakdown, business interruption — are sub-limits adequate?

Property valuation — replacement cost on buildings, agreed value on equipment, scheduled valuables — are values current?

Audit deliverable

A written audit report identifying gaps, recommendations, and priority of action. We rate each item: critical (action required), important (address at renewal), and informational (monitor).

If we find something material that can't wait until renewal, we address it immediately.
