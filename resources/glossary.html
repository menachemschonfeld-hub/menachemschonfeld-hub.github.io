— Insurance glossary

Plain-English definitions of every term you'll see.

Insurance is full of jargon. We've defined the terms you're most likely to encounter on a quote, policy, claim, or renewal — without the legalese.

Actual Cash Value (ACV)
The depreciated value of your property at the time of loss. ACV pays you what your stuff was worth — not what it costs to replace. Less than replacement cost, almost always.
Additional Insured
A party other than the policyholder who is granted coverage under the policy — usually because of a contract requiring it. Common in commercial leases, vendor contracts, and construction subcontracts.
Aggregate Limit
The maximum the policy will pay across all claims during the policy period. Usually 2x the per-claim limit.
Binder
A temporary written confirmation of coverage, issued before the formal policy is delivered.
Business Owners Policy (BOP)
A bundled policy combining property, liability, and business interruption coverage for small-to-mid businesses.
Certificate of Insurance (COI)
A one-page document confirming that a policy is in force. Required by landlords, vendors, and clients to verify your coverage.
Claim
A formal request to your insurance company for payment under your policy after a covered loss.
Claims-Made Policy
A policy that covers claims first reported during the policy period, regardless of when the underlying incident happened. Common in professional liability.
Coinsurance
A property insurance clause requiring you to insure to a specific percentage of replacement cost (usually 80%, 90%, or 100%). If you under-insure, you pay a penalty at the time of loss.
Commercial General Liability (CGL)
The standard third-party liability policy for businesses — covers bodily injury, property damage, and personal injury arising from your operations.
Declarations Page (Dec Page)
The first page of your policy. Lists named insured, limits, deductibles, premium, and effective dates. The most important page to know.
Deductible
The amount you pay out-of-pocket before insurance applies. Higher deductibles = lower premium.
Endorsement
A document modifying your policy. Endorsements add coverage, remove coverage, or change terms. Always read them.
Errors & Omissions (E&O)
Professional liability insurance — coverage for claims arising from your professional advice or services.
Excess Liability
Coverage that sits above an underlying liability policy, providing additional limits.
Exclusion
Something specifically not covered by the policy. Every policy has them. The exclusions are often more important than the covered perils.
Experience Modification (Mod)
A multiplier applied to your workers' comp premium based on your three-year loss history. Average is 1.0; lower is better.
First-Party / Third-Party Coverage
First-party covers your own losses (your property, your medical bills). Third-party covers losses you cause to others (their property, their injury).
Hold Harmless Agreement
A contract clause where one party agrees not to hold another party liable for damages. Often paired with insurance requirements.
Indemnification
One party agreeing to compensate another for loss or damage. Usually contractual, often supported by insurance.
Loss Run
A report from your insurance carrier listing all claims over a specific period (usually 5 years). Required at renewal and when remarketing your account.
Named Insured
The person or entity formally covered by the policy. Subsidiaries, DBAs, and additional entities must be added explicitly.
NFIP
National Flood Insurance Program — the federal flood insurance program. Most flood policies are written through NFIP, though private flood markets exist for higher limits and broader coverage.
Occurrence Policy
A policy that covers incidents that occur during the policy period, regardless of when the claim is reported. Standard for general liability.
Per-Occurrence Limit
The maximum the policy will pay for a single claim or incident.
Premium
The price you pay for the policy.
Replacement Cost
The cost to actually replace damaged property with new property of like kind and quality. Better than ACV.
Reservation of Rights (ROR)
A letter from a carrier saying they're investigating a claim but reserving the right to deny coverage. ROR letters are concerning — call your broker.
Retention
For larger commercial policies (especially professional liability and D&O), the deductible-equivalent. The amount you pay before coverage applies.
Schedule
An attached list of specifically-named items, usually with individual limits — e.g., scheduled jewelry, scheduled equipment, scheduled locations.
Subrogation
The carrier's right to recover from a third party who caused the loss. "Waiver of subrogation" is a common contract requirement.
Surplus Lines
Coverage placed with non-admitted carriers when the standard market won't write the risk. Used for unusual or high-hazard exposures.
Tail Coverage
Extended reporting period for claims-made policies, allowing claims reported after the policy ends but for incidents that occurred during the policy period.
Umbrella
A liability policy sitting above primary policies, providing additional limits and (sometimes) broader coverage.
Underwriting
The carrier's process of evaluating a risk and deciding whether to insure it, on what terms, and at what price.
Waiver of Subrogation
An agreement giving up the carrier's right to recover from a third party. Often required by contract.
